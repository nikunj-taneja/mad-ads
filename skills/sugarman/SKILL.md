# Sugarman: direct-response copy operating system

This skill turns an approved brief into finished copy. It also handles hooks, headlines, VSLs, advertorials, landing pages, and email when no formal brief exists.

The sequence matters:

1. Schwartz determines what kind of promise the market can hear.
2. The Life-Force 8 and means-end chain determine the emotional payoff.
3. Sugarman determines how each sentence pulls the reader into the next.
4. Format and channel determine the output contract and spoken or written register.

Do not reduce this to generic benefit copy. Copy channels existing desire; it does not invent demand or evidence.

## 0. Load the evidence before writing

Read:

- the selected file in `ads/briefed/`;
- `templates/brand.md`, `products.md`, `offers.md`, `brand-voice.md`, and `customer-research.md`;
- `variables/personas.md`, `angles.md`, `formats.md`, `hooks.md`, `usps.md`, `desire-drivers.md`, and `market-sophistication.md`;
- cited reviews, calls, prior ads, or references.

Product codes are internal metadata. Customer-facing copy uses the verified name from `templates/products.md`. Prices, claims, comparisons, testimonials, credentials, urgency, and guarantees require a source path. Insert `[NEEDS INPUT: ...]` when the proof does not exist.

For each brief, extract: product, persona, angle, format, action (`TEST`, `ITERATE`, or `SCALE`), awareness A1-A5, sophistication S1-S5, desire driver, means-end chain, hook direction, beats, proof, objections, offer, CTA, visual direction, guardrails, reference, and length.

## 1. Schwartz strategic pre-flight

### 1.1 Awareness: match the opening to what the prospect already knows

This repository uses an ascending scale.

| Stage | Prospect state | Opening job | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| A1 Unaware | Has not named the problem or desire | Identification, scene, emotion, or attitude | Product name, price, direct claim in the hook |
| A2 Problem-aware | Feels the pain but does not know a solution | Name and dramatize the problem, then reveal a route out | Leading with product features |
| A3 Solution-aware | Wants the category outcome | Name the desire, prove it is achievable, introduce the mechanism | Assuming brand familiarity |
| A4 Product-aware | Knows the product but is unconvinced | New proof, differentiation, objection removal, mechanism | Repeating introductory education |
| A5 Most aware | Knows product and offer | Product, price, incentive, urgency, direct ask | Long story or unnecessary mechanism |

Awareness is not automatically funnel position or targeting. Judge it from the message hypothesis and available evidence.

### 1.2 Sophistication: match the claim to what the market has heard

Read `variables/market-sophistication.md`. Diagnose by claim territory, not country, category, or assumed buyer intelligence.

| Stage | Market condition | Copy move |
|---|---|---|
| S1 | Claim is new | State the benefit directly |
| S2 | Similar claims exist | Enlarge or sharpen the promise with defensible specificity |
| S3 | Claim is commonplace | Introduce and name the mechanism |
| S4 | Mechanisms compete | Elaborate why this mechanism is more complete, easier, or better suited |
| S5 | Market is exhausted | Stop escalating claims; lead with identification, experience, tribe, or a new frame |

Market sophistication and technical sophistication are different. An audience can be claim-burned and still need plain-language mechanism copy.

### 1.3 Pairing rules

- A4-A5: proof, price, incentive, objection resolution. Keep it direct.
- A2-A3 with S3-S4: problem or desire, then mechanism, then product.
- A1 with S5: pure identification and a slow product reveal.
- If the brief and the sophistication registry conflict, flag the mismatch. Use the better-supported diagnosis.

### 1.4 Desire diagnosis: LF8 plus means-end chain

Select one primary Life-Force 8 driver from `variables/desire-drivers.md`. Secondary drivers may ladder upward, but the ad needs one emotional spine.

Write the chain before copy:

`verified attribute -> functional consequence -> personal consequence -> LF8 payoff`

The attribute justifies. The final payoff sells. If the chain breaks, the concept is not ready.

Then write a three-line concept:

- Product: what it physically is.
- Concept: the emotional or identity transformation being sold.
- Link: the familiar category, ritual, role, or reference that makes the concept instantly understandable.

## 2. Sugarman operating axioms

Apply all fifteen:

1. Specific knowledge precedes copy. Study the product and prospect.
2. Every visual and headline exists to earn the first sentence.
3. The first sentence exists to earn the second.
4. Layout and opening create the buying environment before the pitch.
5. Build truthful micro-agreements. Never manipulate with false premises.
6. Every line must make the next line easier to read: the slippery slide.
7. Test inherited assumptions about platform and length instead of treating them as laws.
8. Curiosity sustains attention. Withhold the right information, not necessary truth.
9. Sell a concept, not a collection of product attributes.
10. Incubate important copy, then return with fresh judgment.
11. Length is determined by price, skepticism, awareness, and intent.
12. Write person-to-person, even when the sender is a brand.
13. Anticipate questions in the sequence they arise and resolve them conversationally.
14. Edit until every word performs a job.
15. Active pain is easier to sell than abstract prevention. Reframe prevention as a present cure when truthful.

Sell on emotion and justify with logic. When the product is simple, a credible mechanism can restore interest. When it is complex, simplify the buying idea.

## 3. Seven-step writing process

1. Become expert enough to avoid generic claims.
2. Know the prospect's situation and vocabulary.
3. Draft the concept, headline, and subheadline.
4. Brain-dump the argument without line editing.
5. Edit for logic, rhythm, accuracy, and tightness.
6. Incubate when the task permits.
7. Run the proof, voice, format, and final-action gates.

Before drafting, map objections in order. Each unanswered objection is an exit from the slide.

## 4. Hook discipline

Hooks are written before the body. Produce three meaningfully different candidates using numbered structures from `variables/hooks.md`. Annotate the structure used.

For each candidate:

1. Name one audience emotion: curiosity, fear, frustration, desire, skepticism, urgency, or identification.
2. Choose a matching hook structure. Do not write three paraphrases of one pattern.
3. Fill it with concrete nouns and verified numbers, durations, demonstrations, or comparisons.
4. Match awareness and sophistication.
5. Specify the opening visual for video. Do not assume a talking head is a pattern interrupt.
6. Preserve an open loop the body can honestly close.

Hard failures:

- no evidence or specificity where the hook makes a factual claim;
- claim violates product or legal guardrails;
- stage mismatch, such as product-price copy for A1;
- generic S4-S5 claim with no mechanism or identification move;
- a visual hook that cannot be produced with available assets;
- tension created through deception or fabricated fear.

Soft checks: relevance in the first beat, curiosity, visual legibility without sound, customer-language fit, and a clean handoff to beat two. If all candidates are weak, return to the diagnosis rather than shipping filler.

## 6. The seven body-copy techniques

Map each brief beat to a technique. A useful cold-traffic sequence is often Intensification -> Identification -> Gradualization -> Mechanization -> Concentration -> Camouflage, but the evidence and angle decide.

### 6.1 Intensification

Turn vague desire into varied fulfillment scenes. Put the claim in action, bring the reader into the scene, show change across time, use sourced reactions, picture the cost of the alternative, show ease, and make conviction part of the desired experience. Reinforce from new perspectives instead of repeating synonyms.

### 6.2 Identification

Install a role the prospect wants to inhabit. Character roles are qualities; achievement roles are identities or outcomes. The product becomes a prop in that role. Keep the identity within what the brand and evidence can credibly support.

### 6.3 Gradualization

Build belief rather than demanding it. Use truthful inclusion questions, exact problem identification, causal language, syllogistic sequences, conditional relevance, and varied proof. One fully believed promise beats a pile of weak claims.

### 6.4 Redefinition

Remove built-in objections:

- Simplification reduces perceived effort.
- Escalation reveals a larger but defensible reward.
- Psychological price reduction changes the comparison frame without hiding the actual price.

### 6.5 Mechanization

Answer “how does this work?” at the sophistication level required. At lower stages, naming the mechanism may be enough. At S3, describe it. At S4, elaborate how and why it differs. Price and offer advantages also need believable mechanisms.

### 6.6 Concentration

Make alternatives less attractive through fair comparison. Use bad/good parallel structure or the repeated cycle the buyer wants to escape. Never attack a weakness without immediately supplying the solution, and never invent a competitor flaw.

### 6.7 Camouflage

Match the native trust signals of the medium: creator language for UGC, editorial structure for advertorials, platform-native composition for statics. Camouflage changes presentation, never disclosure or truth.

## 7. LF8 execution mechanics

### 7.1 Mental movie

Climb from vague verb to named situation, visible action, and sensory scene. Use one high-resolution fulfillment scene, usually at the emotional payoff. Sensory detail may dramatize a plausible experience but cannot invent product performance.

### 7.2 Four-walls commitment sequence

For suitable problem or comparison arcs:

1. Isolate a real pain the prospect recognizes.
2. Agitate the current pattern without exaggeration.
3. Establish the desired alternative.
4. Present the product as the logical next step.

Each wall needs an honest yes. Forced agreement destroys trust.

### 7.3 Fear-appeal blueprint

Fear requires four parts in order:

1. Specific, relatable threat to the chosen desire.
2. Clear recommendation.
3. Proof that the recommendation addresses the threat.
4. Self-efficacy: the action feels achievable.

Never raise a threat without a clear escape. Never use shame against protected or deeply personal traits.

### 7.4 Inoculation or preemptive filter

Expose a documented category shortcut, explain why it matters, and show the verified alternative. Attack the practice, not an unsupported caricature of a competitor. This is strongest in comparison, retention, advertorial, and email work.

### 7.5 Direct-response typography decisions

For static and long-form copy: use readable case, short paragraphs, momentum at paragraph openings, and a comfortable measure. Layout, typeface, and hierarchy remain production decisions unless the brief delegates them.

## 9. Spoken register versus written register

Sugarman's print mechanics can sound artificial when spoken. For UGC, founder, product demonstration, or talking-to-camera scripts:

- write one person talking, with connectors such as “and,” “but,” “because,” and “so”;
- vary sentence length materially;
- keep benefit lists inside natural sentences;
- allow one sharp punchline cadence, then return to conversational flow;
- prefer two ideas spoken naturally over four ideas compressed into fragments;
- read aloud and rewrite fragment chains, repeated parallelism, or breathless density.

For static, landing-page, advertorial, and email copy, shorter sentence bursts and deliberate parallelism remain available. Match `templates/brand-voice.md` and actual customer language rather than performing a generic “UGC voice.”

## 10. Output routing by format

### Creator or partnership brief

Do not script a creator's personal testimony. Provide product, length, placement, talking points, hook direction, must mention, must avoid, proof assets, tone, CTA, offer, disclosure, and usage-rights requirements. Only call it a partnership when permission exists.

### Static ad

Write:

- headline, usually no more than eight words;
- optional supporting line;
- CTA;
- visual direction distilled from the brief;
- exact proof or legal copy;
- handoff to `skills/static-producer/SKILL.md`.

For native customer-style statics, provide photo situation, aesthetic register, implied story, and overlay copy. Do not fake a real customer's post.

### Video script

Write three hook candidates, recommend one, then provide a production-ready script with visual direction interleaved at each meaningful beat. Append an end-card specification when the placement uses one. Hand off to `skills/video-producer/SKILL.md`.

### Carousel

Give each card one job. Card one earns the swipe, middle cards advance proof or argument, final card closes. Repeating the same headline across cards is not progression.

## 11. Script construction and close

1. Run awareness, sophistication, desire, and concept diagnosis.
2. Write and gate three hooks.
3. Map beats to body-copy techniques.
4. Deliver the LF8 payoff; use the mechanism as justification.
5. Apply the slippery slide.
6. Place visual direction inline.
7. Place proof exactly where a reasonable reader would demand it.
8. Close with offer summary, price or comparison when relevant, conviction, real urgency, and a direct CTA.
9. Check spoken word count against the requested duration using `tools/check-script-length/check.py`.
10. Run customer voice and read-aloud checks.

Do not hardcode a universal duration or words-per-minute. The brief and production constraints decide, and the tool makes the estimate explicit.

## 12. Six-part Schwartz polish

After the draft:

1. Verification: move proof beside the claim that creates the need for it.
2. Reinforcement: weld adjacent ideas whose combination is stronger.
3. Interweaving: let strong sentences carry promise, image, and logic together.
4. Sensitivity: shift register when attention or belief begins to flatten.
5. Momentum: use transitions and unresolved half-statements to pull forward.
6. Mood: match rhythm to sincerity, drama, authority, intimacy, or urgency without making the technique visible.

## 13. Tactical playbooks

- Opening sequence: curiosity headline, subheadline that extends rather than closes, low-friction first sentence, environment before pitch.
- Anticipate and resolve: name objection in customer language, validate it, resolve factually, restore conviction.
- Satisfaction conviction: express confidence appropriate to the actual policy; never invent risk reversal.
- Specificity upgrade: replace vague benefits with sourced numbers, demonstrations, concrete nouns, or qualified physical detail.
- Linking formula: familiar concept + product = fast understanding. Avoid unlicensed implication or false equivalence.
- Reversal hook: confess a real limitation, then reveal the reason it matters.
- Story first, product later: use when the story itself sustains the slide and the reveal is earned.
- End-of-ad stack: summarize, contextualize price, reinforce conviction, state real urgency, ask directly.
- Read-aloud test: every stumble, skim, or awkward breath marks a leak.

## 14. Channel adaptations

- Short-form paid social: visual and verbal hook immediately, then enough environment to make the reveal meaningful. Interleave edit directions.
- VSL: stack trust, authority, curiosity, story, specificity, mechanism, proof, conviction, urgency, and ask in the order resistance requires.
- Advertorial: editorial promise, readable sections, sourced proof, transparent commercial intent.
- Landing page: one above-fold concept, scannable section heads, involvement language, sequential objection handling, complete close.
- Email: one primary job per email. Welcome may lead with origin; abandonment handles one objection at a time; post-purchase respects consent and relevance.

## 15. Final diagnostic

Before saving, answer:

1. Did the copy assume the correct awareness stage?
2. Is the claim territory's sophistication stage current and sourced?
3. Does the headline make the correct stage move?
4. At S3+, is there a credible mechanism?
5. Is one LF8 payoff visibly connected through a means-end chain?
6. Does every factual claim trace to a file?
7. Is proof placed when demanded?
8. Does every sentence earn the next?
9. Does spoken copy sound spoken?
10. Is the output contract complete for production?

Save to `ads/scripted/<brief-filename>.md`. Include source brief, chosen hook plus alternatives, final copy, production direction, claim citations, unresolved inputs, word-count estimate, and a short rationale naming the awareness/sophistication calibration, LF8 driver, and primary body technique.

In batch mode, continue through resolvable briefs. Mark missing facts inline and summarize gaps at the end. Never fill gaps with plausible copy.
